Description of the workshop including objectives, content, topics of interest:

Networks are emerging as a common language to model a wide variety of systems in life sciences, engineering, and social sciences. Real-world applications give rise to networks that are unstructured and often comprise of multiple-networks. Furthermore, they support multiple dynamical processes that shape the network over time. Network science refers to the broad discipline that seeks to understand the underlying principles that govern the synthesis, analysis and co-evolution of networks.

The workshop will focus on processing large networks. Such networks can be directed as well as undirected, they can be labeled or unlabeled or they can be weighted or unweighted. Furthermore, network of networks is also of interest. Specific scientific topics of interest to the meeting include but are not restricted to: mining for patterns of interest in the networks, efficient exact and approximation algorithms that are either sequential or parallel for analyzing network properties. Recent methods for processing large networks such as map-reduce based frameworks, database techniques for processing networks. A particular topic of interest is to couple structural properties of networks to the dynamics over networks, e.g. contagions.

Description of the format:

Our preference is full-day. But we are also open for a half-day workshop.

The full-day workshop (approximately 6 hours) will feature: 2 invited talks (50 minutes + 10 minutes Q&A), a session (0 minutes) to showcase tools and systems individuals have developed in this area, contributed lectures (approximately 6-8 talks that are 15-20 minutes long) and short session on open problems and directions for future research.

A short description of the target audience:

The workshop is targeted for researchers interested in data mining, machine learning, massive data analytics, network science, social networks and high performance computing in its broadest sense. Both theoreticians as well as practitioners, including system builders and individuals applying network analytic methods in application domains will be benefited from this workshop.

Short biographies of the organizers:

Nitesh Chawla is the Frank Freimann Collegiate Chair of Engineering and the associate professor of computer science and engineering. He is the director of iCeNSA, the university research institute focused on network and data science. He is at the frontier of interdisciplinary applications with innovative work in healthcare analytics, social and information networks, social media, and climate/environmental sciences. He is the recipient of multiple awards for research and teaching innovation. He is the recipient of National Academy of Engineers New Faculty Fellowship, the IBM Watson Faculty Award in 2012, the IBM Big Data and Analytics Faculty Award in 2013, and the IEEE CIS Outstanding Early Career Award in 2014. He serves on editorial boards of a number of journals and organization/program committees of top-tier conferences.

Maleq Khan is a Research Scientist at Network Dynamics and Simulation Science Laboratory (NDSSL), Virginia Bio-Informatics Institute at Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University. His research interests are in network science, parallel algorithms, distributed algorithms, graph algorithms, data mining, and communication networks. He obtained his BS in Computer Science and Engineering in 1996 from the Bangladesh Univeristy of Engineering and Technology (BUET), Dhaka, Bnagladesh. After obtiaing his BS, Maleq worked as lecturer in universities for several years. Then he obtained his Ph.D. in Computer Science in 2007 from Purdue University, West Lafayette, Indiana. Maleq has published over 30 papers in peer-reviewed conferences and journals in his area of expertise. He has won a best paper award at DISC 2006. He has served in the program committees of several conferences and workshops.

Madhav Marathe is a Professor, of Computer Science, and the Director of Network Dynamics and Simulation Science Laboratory (NDSSL), Virginia Bio-Informatics Institute at Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University. His research interests are in network science, design and analysis of algorithms, computational complexity, communication networks and high performance computing. He obtained his B.Tech degree in 1989 in Computer Science and Engineering from the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Madras, and his Ph.D. in 1994 in Computer Science, from University at Albany. Before coming to Virginia Tech, he was a Team Leader in the Computer and Computational Sciences division at the Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL) where he led the basic research programs in foundations of computing and high performance simulation science for analyzing extremely large socio-technical and critical infrastructure networks. He is a fellow of the ACM and the IEEE, and he was the inaugural George Michael Fellow at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory.
